Our Mission

To assist nature in creating a natural carbon capture ecosystem that will hold a minimum of 10% of the man made CO2 that is created on the globe each year. Because our carbon capture mechanism is 100% natural tree based the tree’s we plant will all serve as habitat for animals, insects and can provide future materials for wood products that can be harvested in a sustainable manner for use in building homes and furniture for people throughout the United States and around the globe.

Planet Green is not meant to be to be a substitute for other carbon reduction or sequestration programs but rather a supplement to them. The timeline for completion of this project is the year 2030 which the UN has estimated is the maximum timeframe for earth’s inhabitants to reach a carbon neutral balance for the planet.

Planet Green will utilize the following strategy to plant 25,000 trees each year in Walnut creek for the next 12 years and 110MM in the United States until we have reached a goal of planting 1.32BB trees in the United States.

  • We are asking each city to designate land within the their respective city or county sufficient for each of its inhabitants to plant 10 trees per year per citizen. The tree’s selected for each city will be indigenous tree’s that are specified by each city as “ok to plant”. Once the tree’s are planted we are asking for the cities to take responsibility to water the tree’s each year until a sufficient root system enables the tree’s to self maintain their water supply.
  • We are asking for the United States government to allot a tax credit for individuals who produce a receipt for tree’s that are purchased and planted such that individuals are 100% reimbursed for their the cost of the tree’s.

The goal is 10 tree’s per person per year for the next 12 years. This will eventually sequester 5% of the globs carbon and in doing so provide habitat for smaller plants, insets, birds and other animals. Tree’s also provide much needed soil stability for weather events that can create mudslides or hill slippage.

This project isn’t about “me” or “you” it’s about “us” and our planet and the habitability of “our” planet 12 years from today and 120 years from tomorrow. According to the United Nations our planet is in peril.

Here is that the United Nations has to say:

We have just 12 years to make massive and unprecedented changes to global energy infrastructure to limit global warming to moderate levels, the United Nation’s climate science body said in a monumental new report released Sunday.

"There is no documented historic precedent” for the action needed at this moment, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) wrote in its 700-page report on the impacts of global warming of 2.7 degrees Fahrenheit, or 1.5 degrees Celsius.

From rising sea levels to more devastating droughts to more damaging storms, the report makes brutally clear that warming will make the world worse for us in the forms of famine, disease, economic tolls, and refugee crises. And there is a vast gulf between the devastation from 1.5°C, what’s considered the moderate level of average warming, and 2°C.

“It’s very clear that half a degree matters,” said Valérie Masson-Delmotte, co-chair of IPCC Working Group I at a press conference in Incheon, South Korea, where the report was released.

Under the Paris climate agreement, nations set a goal of limiting warming to 3.6°F, or 2 °C, increase in global average temperatures, with ambitions of a stricter limit of 2.7°F, or 1.5°C of warming. The UN asked the IPCC to figure out what it would take to hit the 1.5°C target, and what’s in store for the world if we did pull it off.

The team pooled more than 6,000 scientific publications, drew contributions from 133 authors, and had more than 1,000 scientists review the findings.

As expected, the report doesn’t pull any punches: Staying at or below 1.5°C requires slashing global greenhouse gas emissions 45 percent below 2010 levels by 2030 and reaching net zero by 2050.

Meeting this goal demands extraordinary transitions in transportation; in energy, land, and building infrastructure; and in industrial systems. It means reducing our current coal consumption by one-third. It also demands a vast scale-up of emerging technologies, such as those that remove carbon dioxide directly from the air. All in the very narrow window of the next 12 years while our momentum pushes us in the wrong direction.
